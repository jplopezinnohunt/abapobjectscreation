The Story: Opening a New Bank Account

UNESCO has just opened a new bank account at UBA Mozambique in Maputo. The physical account exists at the bank. Money can be deposited into it. But SAP doesn't know it exists yet.

Until SAP is configured, this account is invisible. No bank statement can be processed. No payment can be sent through it. Treasury can't see its balance. Month-end revaluation skips it. Reconciliation is impossible.

13 configuration steps bring this account to life inside SAP. Each step enables a specific capability. Miss one, and that capability doesn't work — sometimes silently, which is worse than a hard error.

This companion explains each step in business language: what it does, why it matters, and what breaks without it.

The 13 Configuration Steps — In Business Language

Each step is explained functionally first. Technical references are at the bottom of each card in muted text. Read the "What" and "Why" to understand the purpose; skip the tech reference unless you need to configure it yourself.

Step 1 — Foundation

Creating the Bank Accounts in SAP

Creates two G/L accounts per bank — one for the actual bank balance (10* range), and one for payments in transit (11* range). Think of it like having a main account and a clearing account in your personal banking.

The main account (10*) records the bank's true balance: deposits, withdrawals, fees, interest. The clearing account (11*) tracks payments that SAP has sent but the bank hasn't confirmed yet.

If missing: There's literally nowhere to record transactions. Every posting needs a destination G/L account. Without these, no bank statement can be processed, no payment can be posted.
ALL CAPABILITIES
The clearing account (11*) has Open Item Management enabled — this is what makes reconciliation possible. Each outgoing payment creates an "open item" on 11*. When the bank statement confirms the payment cleared, that item gets matched and closed. If it doesn't match, it stays open for investigation.

Step 2 — Foundation

Registering the Bank in SAP

Tells SAP "we have a bank account at UBA Mozambique, here's the account number, SWIFT code, and address." Like adding a new bank to your phone's banking app — you're registering its identity so the system can reference it.

This creates the "house bank" and "account ID" combination that every other configuration step references.

If missing: SAP doesn't know this bank exists. Every subsequent step references the house bank ID. Without it, nothing else can be configured.
ALL CAPABILITIES
The DME instruction key (e.g., B2) controls the format of payment files sent to the bank. Getting this wrong means the bank rejects payment files — the payments stay in SAP but never actually reach the bank.

Step 3 — Monitoring

Bank Statement Monitor

Adds this account to the daily monitoring dashboard. Treasury can see at a glance whether today's bank statement from UBA Maputo arrived and was processed successfully.

The monitor shows a traffic-light status for each configured account: green (received and posted), yellow (received but errors), red (not received).

If missing: A missing bank statement goes unnoticed. The bank might have sent it, but it failed during import — nobody would know until reconciliation gaps appear weeks later. By then, the trail is cold.

Step 4 — Month-End

Exchange Rate Configuration

Tells SAP which accounts to use when posting exchange rate gains or losses. Only needed for non-USD accounts (MZN in the case of UBA Mozambique).

At month-end, all foreign currency balances are revalued at the current exchange rate. If UBA held 50M MZN last month when the rate was 63.5 MZN/USD, and this month it's 64.0 MZN/USD, that's a loss. SAP calculates it and posts it — but it needs to know where to post it.

If missing: The revaluation program (FAGL_FC_VAL) fails for this account. The foreign currency balance is never adjusted, financial statements show stale exchange rates, and the balance sheet is inaccurate.
CLOSE
You must fill ALL 3 sections: Realized gains/losses + Valuation differences + Correction accounts. If you only fill Realized, month-end revaluation is incomplete. This mistake was discovered during UBA01 configuration by comparing with the existing ECO09 bank setup.

Step 5 — Identity

Cash Management Name

Gives the account a short, human-readable name like "UBA01-USD1" that appears in cash management reports and bank statement processing screens.

Without it, Treasury sees a raw G/L number (1065421) instead of a meaningful name. When you have 90+ house banks, that makes reports unreadable.

If missing: The account shows as a cryptic G/L number in all reports. Treasury can't quickly identify which bank it is when scanning daily cash positions.

Step 6 — Critical

Electronic Bank Statement Setup

This is actually two configurations in one screen, and missing either one is a common mistake:

Part A (T035D): "When a statement arrives for account UBA01-USD1, post to G/L account 1065421." This is the mapping from the house bank account ID to the actual G/L account number.

Part B (T028B): "When we receive a file from bank key SP0000001YCB with account number 070340000190, it's an MT940 format and belongs to UBA01-USD1." This is how SAP identifies the physical bank file.

If Part A missing: SAP knows the file belongs to UBA01 but doesn't know which G/L account to post to. Statement import fails.
If Part B missing: SAP doesn't even recognize the file. The MT940 arrives but SAP says "I don't know this bank account" and rejects it entirely.
RECEIVE RECONCILE
These are TWO different tables (T035D and T028B) configured in the SAME SPRO screen. Having one without the other is a common trap. During UBA01 configuration, T028B was missing while T035D was present — the bank statement arrived but SAP couldn't identify which bank sent it.

Step 7 — Accounting Control

Business Area Assignment

Ensures bank postings are automatically assigned to the correct fund (GEF = General Fund). UNESCO uses business areas to track which fund owns each transaction.

All bank accounts in the range 1000000–1199999 are covered by an existing rule, so new accounts in this range are automatically included.

If missing: Postings would lack a business area, requiring manual intervention or failing budget checks during period close.
ALL CAPABILITIES Usually N/A — range rule covers automatically
Transaction: YFI_BASU_MOD  |  Rule: G/L range 1000000–1199999 → Business Area GEF
Step 8 — Internal Transfers

Receiving Bank Clearing

When money arrives at UBA Mozambique from another UNESCO bank account (an internal transfer), SAP needs to know which clearing account to use for the receiving side.

Internal transfers are two-sided: the sending bank debits its clearing account, and the receiving bank needs to clear on its side too.

If missing: Internal transfers post on the sending side but the receiving side doesn't clear properly. The receiving bank shows unmatched open items that should have cleared automatically.

Step 9 — Payment Engine

Payment Program Setup

This is actually three sub-steps that make the bank available for outgoing payments:

9.1 — Register as Paying Bank (T042I): Tell the payment program (F110) "you can use UBA01 for MZN payments, and use clearing account 1165424."

9.2 — Internal Transfer Method: Add to the internal transfer payment method so UNESCO-to-UNESCO transfers can use this bank.

9.3 — Payment File Template (OBPM4): Create the variant that tells SAP how to generate the XML file in the format UBA accepts.

If 9.1 missing: F110 will never select this bank for payments — it doesn't know it exists as a paying bank. Invoices due in MZN will fail to find a bank.
If 9.3 missing: Even if F110 selects the bank, no payment file is generated. The payment stays in SAP but never reaches the bank. The vendor doesn't get paid.
PAY
OBPM4 variants must be created in V01 AND P01 separately — they are NOT transportable. If you only create them in D01, the payment file generation works in development but fails silently in production.

Treasury uses this to run the Average Balance report — how much money sits in each bank on average over a period.

If missing: The account is invisible to the Average Balance report. Treasury might not know there's $2M sitting idle in Maputo when they're looking for funds to deploy.

Step 13 — Payments

IBAN Generation

Registers the IBAN (International Bank Account Number) in SAP. This is what appears on payment documents and enables SEPA and international transfers through the SWIFT network.

The IBAN is the globally standardized format that intermediary banks use to route payments. Without it, a payment from UNESCO HQ to UBA Mozambique might be rejected by an intermediary bank in the chain.

If missing: International payments might be rejected by intermediary banks. SWIFT/BIC alone is not sufficient for many payment networks, especially in Europe and increasingly in Africa.
PAY
Table: TIBAN  |  Key: Bank country + bank key + account number → IBAN

Bank Statements — How Money Comes In

Every morning at 7:00 AM, UBA Mozambique sends a file to UNESCO. This file, in MT940 format (a SWIFT banking standard), contains every transaction from yesterday: deposits received, payments confirmed, fees charged, interest credited.

SAP picks up the file automatically from a shared folder. Then it asks three questions:

The Three Questions SAP Asks

QUESTION 1: "Who sent this?" The MT940 file header contains the bank's physical account information: Bank key: SP0000001YCB Account: 070340000190 SAP looks this up in T028B (Electronic Bank Statement mapping): SP0000001YCB + 070340000190 → House Bank: UBA01 Account ID: USD1 Format: XRT940 If T028B is not configured: SAP says "I don't know this bank account" and rejects the entire file. The statement sits unprocessed. Nobody is notified unless Step 3 (Monitor) is set up. QUESTION 2: "Where do I post it?" Now SAP knows this is UBA01-USD1. But which G/L account? SAP looks this up in T035D (Account determination): House Bank UBA01 + Account USD1 → G/L Account: 1065421 If T035D is not configured: SAP knows the file is from UBA01 but has nowhere to post. Import fails with "No account determination found." QUESTION 3: "How do I categorize each line?" Each transaction in the MT940 has a type code (NTRF = transfer, NCHG = charge, etc.) SAP looks up posting rules in T028G: Format XRT940 + Code NTRF + Direction "-" → Rule: SUBD Algorithm: 015 The posting rule determines: - Which side is debited/credited - Whether to attempt automatic clearing - What document type to use Each line becomes a journal entry in SAP.

The T035D + T028B Relationship

These two tables work together but serve different purposes. Both are configured in the same SPRO screen, which makes it easy to configure one and forget the other.

The Reconciliation Flow

This is where the clearing account (11*) does its magic:

DAY 1: Payment Goes Out Treasury runs F110 to pay a vendor in Maputo. SAP creates the payment: DEBIT Vendor account (clears the invoice) CREDIT Clearing account 1165424 (11*) ↑ This creates an OPEN ITEM It means: "We told the bank to pay, but the bank hasn't confirmed yet." DAY 2: Bank Statement Arrives The MT940 file contains a debit line: "Payment to vendor XYZ, 500,000 MZN" SAP processes the statement: DEBIT Bank account 1065421 (10*) — bank balance goes down CREDIT Clearing account 1165424 (11*) — matches and CLEARS the open item Result: The open item from Day 1 is now closed. The payment is fully reconciled. WHAT IF IT DOESN'T MATCH? If the bank charged a fee (500,000 MZN sent, but bank debited 500,150 MZN): The amounts don't match → the open item stays on 1165424 It appears in FEBAN (Electronic Bank Statement processing) Treasury investigates and either: - Posts the fee separately and clears manually - Adjusts the tolerance and re-runs clearing

Payments — How Money Goes Out

A vendor in Maputo delivered office supplies and sent an invoice for 500,000 MZN. The invoice has been approved, entered into SAP, and its payment due date has arrived.

Treasury runs the Automatic Payment Program (F110) — this is the engine that processes all due payments across UNESCO's global operations.

The Payment Journey

STEP 1: F110 SCANS FOR DUE INVOICES The payment program looks at all vendor invoices where: - Payment due date has arrived (or is within the run's horizon) - Invoice is approved and not blocked - Vendor is not on a payment hold It finds: Vendor 12345 — 500,000 MZN — due today STEP 2: SELECT THE PAYING BANK F110 asks: "Which bank can pay 500,000 MZN to a vendor in Mozambique?" It looks in T042I (Paying bank configuration): Payment method T (bank transfer) + Currency MZN → House Bank: UBA01 Account: MZN01 → Clearing account: 1165424 If T042I is not configured for UBA01: F110 says "No bank found for MZN payments" and the invoice goes unpaid. The vendor calls. Treasury scrambles. STEP 3: POST THE PAYMENT SAP creates the accounting entry: DEBIT Vendor 12345 — 500,000 MZN (clears the open invoice) CREDIT Clearing account 1165424 — 500,000 MZN (creates open item) The invoice is now marked as "paid" in SAP. But the money hasn't actually left the bank yet. The open item on 1165424 means: "We promised to pay, bank hasn't executed yet." STEP 4: GENERATE THE PAYMENT FILE SAP runs the OBPM4 variant for UBA01: Format: /GGI_XML_CT_UNESCO (XML credit transfer) Output: An XML file containing: - Sender: UNESCO UBA account - Beneficiary: Vendor 12345 - Amount: 500,000 MZN - Bank routing details If OBPM4 variant is missing: The payment is posted in SAP (vendor shows "paid") But NO file is generated. The bank never receives the instruction. The vendor never gets the money. The open item on 1165424 never clears. This is the worst failure mode — SAP thinks it's done, but nothing happened. STEP 5: SEND TO BANK VIA BCM The XML file is picked up by BCM (Bank Communication Management): - File is signed with UNESCO's digital certificate - Transmitted to UBA via secure channel (SWIFT or EBICS) - BCM tracks the file status: sent, acknowledged, rejected If the bank rejects the file (wrong format, invalid IBAN, etc.): BCM shows the rejection reason Treasury must fix and resend STEP 6: BANK EXECUTES THE PAYMENT UBA Mozambique processes the transfer. The vendor receives 500,000 MZN in their bank account. STEP 7: NEXT-DAY RECONCILIATION Next morning, the MT940 statement arrives. It contains a debit: "Transfer to vendor 12345 — 500,000 MZN" SAP matches this against the open item on 1165424: Amount matches → Item cleared automatically The cycle is complete. The payment is fully reconciled.

Monthly Close: FX Revaluation

At month-end, UBA Mozambique holds 50,000,000 MZN. Last month, the exchange rate was 63.5 MZN per USD, making this balance worth approximately $787,402 USD. This month, the rate moved to 64.0 MZN/USD, making it worth $781,250 USD.

That's a $6,152 unrealized loss due to exchange rate movement. SAP must calculate and post this difference so the balance sheet reflects the true USD-equivalent value.

FX REVALUATION PROCESS (FAGL_FC_VAL) 1. Program reads all foreign currency G/L balances 2. For each account, it calculates: Local balance: 50,000,000 MZN Old rate: 63.5 MZN/USD → $787,402 New rate: 64.0 MZN/USD → $781,250 Difference: -$6,152 (unrealized loss) 3. Posts the adjustment: DEBIT FX Loss account (from T030H config) $6,152 CREDIT Balance sheet adjustment account $6,152 If OBA1 (Step 4) is not configured for 1065421: Program skips this account entirely. Balance sheet shows stale exchange rate. Financial statements are inaccurate. Common mistake: Only filling "Realized" gains/losses in T030H. Must fill ALL 3: Realized + Valuation + Correction. Discovered during UBA01 config by comparing with ECO09.

Reconciliation: FEBAN

FEBAN (Electronic Bank Statement Post-Processing) is where Treasury investigates items that didn't clear automatically.

Situation What Shows in FEBAN Action Required
Payment matches exactly Nothing (auto-cleared) None

Payment rejected by bank Open item on clearing account, no matching bank debit Investigate rejection, resend or reverse
Unknown deposit received Bank credit with no matching SAP document Research and post manually
Statement not received Shows in FTE_BSM monitor (Step 3) Contact bank, re-request statement
